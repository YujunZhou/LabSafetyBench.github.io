Introduction

Artificial Intelligence (AI) is rapidly transforming scientific research, reshaping discovery across disciplines. However, its growing use in laboratory settings introduces critical safety challenges. Overreliance on AI can create an ‚Äúillusion of understanding,‚Äù where flawed or incomplete recommendations may lead to hazardous decisions.

Laboratory accidents‚Äîincluding explosions, chemical spills, and radiation leaks‚Äîunderscore the urgent need for rigorous safety protocols. To address these challenges, we propose LabSafety Bench, a comprehensive benchmark that evaluates both large language models (LLMs) and vision language models (VLMs) on their ability to identify hazards, assess risks, and predict the consequences of unsafe actions in lab environments.

LabSafety Bench comprises 765 multiple-choice questions (632 text-only and 133 text-with-image) aligned with US OSHA protocols, along with 520 realistic laboratory scenarios that generate a total of 4,090 open-ended questions. Our evaluations across eight proprietary models, seven open-weight LLMs, and four VLMs reveal that even state-of-the-art models fall short of the safety threshold required for reliable operation.

Our Contributions: (1) We establish the first benchmark focused on the trustworthiness of AI in high-stakes lab safety environments. (2) We introduce a dual-task framework that assesses both structured (multiple-choice) knowledge and practical, scenario-based decision-making. (3) Our comprehensive evaluations highlight significant gaps in current models‚Äô safety awareness, emphasizing the need for improved training and specialized evaluation frameworks.

LabSafety Bench Dataset

Overview

LabSafety Bench is a comprehensive evaluation framework designed to rigorously assess the safety awareness and decision-making capabilities of LLMs/VLMs in laboratory settings. Our approach begins with a new taxonomy for lab safety grounded in OSHA protocols, ensuring thorough coverage of potential hazards.

Through expert-AI collaboration, we curate a dataset comprising 765 multiple-choice questions‚Äîdivided into 632 text-only and 133 text-with-image questions‚Äîand 520 realistic laboratory scenarios, yielding a total of 4,090 open-ended questions. While the multiple-choice questions test structured knowledge, the scenario-based tasks challenge models to both identify hazards and predict the consequences of unsafe actions.

This dual-task design enables a holistic evaluation of both factual knowledge and practical decision-making in high-stakes lab environments.
